Key Findings

1. High-Intensity Running Is a Primary Driver
Time-in-zone analysis reveals that during your peak VO2 max period (Oct–Nov 2025), your 17 running sessions averaged 55% Zone 3, 24% Zone 4, and 12% Zone 5. Combined Zone 4+5 minutes correlate at r = +0.68 (30-day). The earlier workout-average approach masked this because easy walking sessions diluted the numbers. Running volume and intensity are major factors for your VO2 max.
2. Strength Training Provides a Strong Foundation
Traditional strength training minutes show r = +0.56 with VO2 max — the strongest single workout-type correlation. During your peak, you averaged 16 sessions of strength training over the two months. Strength training builds muscular efficiency that supports cardiovascular performance, and in your data, both session frequency and total minutes contribute independently.
3. Heart Rate Recovery Tracks Fitness Closely
Your 1-minute heart rate recovery is the strongest individual health metric correlation (r = +0.73 over 30 days). During your peak it averaged 40.7 bpm vs 33.2 in the baseline period. This serves as both a leading indicator and a result of improving cardiovascular fitness — useful for tracking progress between VO2 max measurements.
4. Polarized Training Distribution Works Best
Your peak period shows a polarized pattern: ~55% Zone 1, minimal Zone 2, and ~39% in Zone 3–5 (driven by intense runs). The "polarization index" (Z1+Z5 as % of total) correlates at r = +0.54. Zone 3 time alone shows a weak-to-moderate positive correlation (r = +0.40), while the middle zones as a proportion of training are less beneficial than the extremes.
5. Volume Matters — But Intensity Matters More
Your recent training (Feb–Mar 2026) has the highest volume ever at 543 min/week — but 76% is Zone 1 with almost no Zone 4–5 time. VO2 max has dropped from 46.6 to 43.3. Compare this to your peak period at just 333 min/week but with 49 minutes of Zone 4+5 weekly. More isn't better if the intensity profile shifts too easy.
6. Yoga Volume Inversely Correlates (Displacement Effect)
Yoga minutes show r = –0.51 with VO2 max. This doesn't mean yoga is harmful — rather, periods where yoga dominates your training at the expense of cardio and strength tend to coincide with lower VO2 max. Yoga is fine for recovery and mobility, but it shouldn't crowd out higher-intensity training.

Actionable Recommendations

1. Restore Zone 4–5 Running Volume
Your current training has dropped to near-zero Zone 4–5 time. During your peak you logged ~49 min/week in these zones (r = +0.68 with VO2 max). Reintroduce 2–3 hard running sessions per week with tempo intervals or threshold runs to recover the intensity stimulus that drove your best numbers.
2. Maintain Strength Training Consistency
Keep 2–3 traditional strength sessions per week. Both frequency (r = +0.52) and total minutes (r = +0.56) contribute independently to VO2 max. Your peak period had ~14 strength sessions over 8 weeks.
3. Track Heart Rate Recovery
Monitor your 1-minute HR recovery as a leading indicator (r = +0.73). At your peak it averaged 41 bpm; during lower fitness periods it dropped to 33. If recovery starts declining, it signals VO2 max may follow — a useful early warning to adjust training.
4. Aim for a Polarized Training Distribution
Your best results came from ~55% Zone 1 + ~39% Zone 3–5 with very little Zone 2 (the "gray zone"). Keep your easy days truly easy and your hard days genuinely hard. The current pattern of high volume with everything in Zone 1–2 isn't as effective as less total volume with sharper intensity contrast.
5. Prioritize REM Sleep Quality
Among sleep metrics, REM sleep hours showed the clearest positive contribution in the regression model (β = +0.57 standardized). Total sleep hours had a slight negative coefficient, suggesting quality matters more than quantity. Consistent sleep schedules and limiting late alcohol can improve REM proportion.
6. Watch Walking Heart Rate as a Passive Signal
Average walking heart rate is strongly inversely correlated (r = –0.59). A higher HR during casual walking reflects lower cardiovascular efficiency. This requires no extra testing — just watch the trend in the Health app. If it creeps up, it may signal detraining.

Methodology Notes

Data was extracted from 4GB Apple Health export.xml via streaming XML parser. All metrics were aggregated to daily level and combined using 7, 14, and 30-day rolling averages. VO2 max readings (n=866 daily, 1,208 total) served as the target variable. Analysis methods: (1) Pearson correlation across all feature/VO2max pairs; (2) Forward stepwise linear regression selecting 20 features for a final R² = 0.80; (3) 100-tree random decision ensemble for non-linear feature importance; (4) Heart rate zone analysis using 1.3M individual HR samples matched to 1,383 workout windows via binary search, with zones calculated from observed max HR of 197 bpm (99th percentile cluster from Jul 2025 session). Time-in-zone was estimated by assigning each HR sample a duration equal to the gap to the next reading, capped at 30 seconds. The 220-minus-age formula was found to underestimate max HR by 15 bpm, which would have significantly misclassified zone distributions.
