Key Findings

1. Sleep Quality Is Hard to Predict From Daytime Activity
The regression models for sleep metrics have low R² values — total sleep R² = 0.09, deep sleep R² = 0.08, REM sleep R² = 0.04. This means daytime exercise, heart rate, and activity metrics explain less than 10% of your nightly sleep variation. Sleep is influenced heavily by factors not captured in Health data: stress, caffeine, screen time, light exposure, room temperature, and mental state.
2. HIIT Is Your Best Workout for Deep Sleep
Days with HIIT sessions are followed by +0.12h (7 min) more deep sleep and +0.24h (14 min) more total sleep on average. This was the strongest workout-type effect on deep sleep. HIIT drives physical fatigue that promotes slow-wave (deep) sleep recovery. Functional strength training also showed a +0.09h deep sleep boost.
3. Afternoon Workouts Produce the Best Sleep
Afternoon exercise (12–6pm) is associated with +15 min total sleep and +4 min REM compared to no-workout days. Evening workouts also show a positive effect (+13 min total), while morning workouts are associated with slightly less sleep (–12 min). This may reflect that morning workouts coincide with earlier alarms cutting sleep short.
4. Strength Training Has a Mixed Sleep Effect
Traditional strength training days are followed by 12 min less total sleep and 5 min less deep sleep — but 21% higher sleep efficiency. You sleep less but more efficiently. This pattern suggests strength training may not impair sleep quality per se, but might coincide with days where you go to bed later or have a shorter sleep window.
5. You Sleep Best on Weekends and in Spring
Saturday nights average 8.1h (vs 7.5h Thursday nights) with the most REM (1.95h). Seasonally, April–May is your best sleep period at 8.3h average with the most deep sleep (1.1h). December–January is your worst at 7.6h. Longer daylight hours and warmer weather appear to support better sleep.
6. Running Slightly Reduces Deep Sleep
Days with running are followed by 5 fewer minutes of deep sleep on average, despite dramatically higher sleep efficiency (+21%). This might reflect that running's cardiovascular stress shifts sleep architecture toward lighter recovery patterns. The higher efficiency suggests you fall asleep faster and stay asleep, but spend less time in the deepest stage.

Recommendations

1. Schedule Hard Workouts in the Afternoon
Your data shows afternoon workouts (12–6pm) produce the best sleep outcomes — more total sleep and more REM. If you're doing HIIT or intense sessions, afternoon timing appears optimal for sleep quality.
2. Use HIIT and Functional Strength for Deep Sleep
HIIT (+12 min deep) and functional strength (+9 min deep) are your best workout types for deep sleep. If you're prioritizing recovery and deep sleep, incorporate these 2–3 times per week.
3. Don't Worry About Evening Exercise
Contrary to common advice, your data shows evening workouts are associated with more total sleep (+13 min) and better efficiency. Exercise close to bedtime isn't disrupting your sleep — in fact, it may be helping.
4. Look Beyond Exercise for Sleep Optimization
With R² under 0.10 for all sleep targets, exercise explains less than 10% of your sleep variation. The biggest levers are likely not in Health data: consistent bedtime routine, managing stress, limiting caffeine after 2pm, reducing screen time before bed, and keeping your bedroom cool and dark.
5. Leverage Weekend Patterns
You average 8.1h on Saturday nights vs 7.5h on Thursdays. Understanding what's different about weekends — likely less alarm-driven wake-ups and lower stress — could help you design weeknight routines that capture some of that benefit.
6. Monitor Respiratory Rate as a Sleep Signal
Respiratory rate was the strongest health-metric correlate with sleep efficiency (r = –0.32). Lower respiratory rates during sleep reflect better cardiovascular fitness and are associated with higher quality sleep. This connects back to your VO2 max work — better cardio fitness → lower respiratory rate → better sleep efficiency.

Methodology

Sleep data from 63,639 records across Apple Watch and Eight Sleep, aggregated to nightly metrics. Stage-level analysis (core, deep, REM, awake) limited to Sep 2022–Mar 2026 (n=1,113 nights) when Apple Watch began reporting sleep stages. Features were drawn from the previous day's activity, exercise, heart rate, and body metrics. Analysis methods: Pearson correlation, forward stepwise regression (15 features max), and direct comparison of workout-day vs non-workout-day sleep metrics. Sleep efficiency defined as total asleep time / total time in bed.

Important caveat R² values are low (0.04–0.09), meaning exercise and health metrics explain only a small portion of sleep variation. Sleep is a complex behavior influenced by psychological, environmental, and behavioral factors largely absent from Apple Health data.
