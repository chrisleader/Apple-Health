Sleep Efficiency Is Most Predictable
R² = 0.44 for sleep efficiency, compared to 0.12 for total sleep, 0.12 for deep sleep, and 0.11 for REM sleep. Sleep quality markers (SpO₂, activity, daylight, consistency) explain efficiency variation much better than sleep duration.

Key Findings

Blood Oxygen Is the #1 Sleep Quality Marker
SpO₂ minimum r = +0.33 with efficiency, SpO₂ variability r = −0.28. Good breathing patterns correlate strongly with better sleep quality.
Daily Activity Drives Sleep Efficiency
Workout count r = +0.32, stand hours r = +0.31, active energy r = +0.30. Any movement throughout the day improves sleep quality.
Daylight Exposure Boosts Sleep Duration
High daylight days: 8.13h sleep, 1.06h deep. Low daylight days: 7.80h, 0.96h deep. Average 75 min/day, median 55 min. Natural light exposure adds ~20 min to total sleep.
HIIT Is the Champion for Deep Sleep
HIIT workouts add +12 min deep sleep and +14 min total sleep. Largest effect size of any workout type, including strength training and running.
Afternoon Workouts Are the Sweet Spot
Afternoon exercise adds +15 min sleep AND +13% efficiency. Morning workouts boost efficiency (+16%) but reduce total duration (−11 min).
Sleep Duration Remains Hard to Predict
R² = 0.12 means 88% remains unexplained. Stress, caffeine, screens, room temperature, and circadian factors dominate. Apple Health captures efficiency well but not duration.

Physiological Deep Dives

Blood Oxygen (SpO₂) Dynamics
Minimum SpO₂ r = +0.33 with efficiency; variability (std dev) r = −0.28. Stable, higher oxygen levels during sleep correlate with better sleep quality. May indicate undiagnosed sleep apnea in low-minimum nights.
Daylight Exposure
Average 75 min/day (median 55 min). High daylight days yield 8.13h sleep vs 7.80h on low daylight days. +20 min/day difference. Critical for circadian rhythm regulation.
Breathing Disturbances
Mean 0.79/hr across 455 nights. Counterintuitive positive correlation with REM sleep, likely reflects normal REM-stage breathing variability rather than pathology.
Evening Noise
Average 63.3 dB (evening). Positive correlation with deep sleep (r = +0.15) likely reflects social engagement and daytime activity rather than actual noise disruption.
Mindfulness & Meditation
Only 86/1,026 days recorded (8.4%). Shows −0.15h sleep but +0.09 REM%. Likely confounded by high-stress days driving both increased meditation and fragmented sleep.
Dietary Factors
Only ~74 days tracked. Carbs r = +0.11 total sleep, water r = +0.09, vitamin D r = +0.15 deep sleep. Limited data; correlations are suggestive only.

Recommendations

Monitor Blood Oxygen
#1 efficiency predictor (r = +0.33). Track SpO₂ minimum and variability. If minimum regularly drops below 92%, consider sleep apnea screening with healthcare provider.
Get More Daylight
Target 75+ minutes daily. High daylight days yield +20 min sleep and +6 min deep sleep. Morning light exposure is especially effective for circadian rhythm.
Exercise in the Afternoon
Afternoon workouts add +15 min sleep AND +13% efficiency. Morning exercises boost efficiency but reduce duration; evening workouts are neutral.
Prioritize HIIT for Deep Sleep
HIIT adds +12 min deep sleep per session, the largest effect of any workout type. Running is best for efficiency; HIIT for deep sleep duration.
Stay Consistent with Bedtimes
Bedtime consistency is a top-10 efficiency predictor.±30 min variance is acceptable; ±2+ hours significantly reduces sleep quality.
Plan for Seasonal Dips
April–May peak at 8.3h sleep, 1.1h deep. December–January trough at 7.6h, 0.90h deep. Expect and adjust activity/daylight in winter months.
Any Workout Beats None
Consistency matters more than type. Even 10 min of walking (r = +0.28) or light strength training (r = +0.11) improves efficiency over no activity.
Watch Morning Workouts
Morning exercise boosts efficiency (+16%) but reduces total duration (−11 min). If aiming for longer sleep, shift to afternoon/evening; if aiming for quality, mornings work.

Caveats & Data Limitations

  • Dietary Data: Only ~74 days of logging (7.2%). Correlations suggestive, not reliable for decision-making.
  • SpO₂ Units: Stored as decimal fraction (0–1.0), not percentage. All values are typical for healthy individuals.
  • Wrist Temperature: Mean 96.7°F. Limited variation; no strong predictive signal.
  • Evening Noise Correlation: Positive association (r = +0.15 with deep sleep) likely reflects social engagement and daytime activity, not actual noise disruption.
  • Mindfulness Sessions: Only 86 days (8.4%). Confounded by high-stress periods driving both increased meditation and fragmented sleep.
  • Breathing Disturbances: Only 455 nights (41.8%) with data. Positive REM correlation is normal; not a pathology indicator.
  • Model Explains 44% of Sleep Efficiency, 12% of Duration. 56–88% driven by unmeasured factors: stress, caffeine, screen time, ambient temperature, partner disruption, illness.
